One has to be willing to engage in an experimental quest of trial and error to participate in the building of the World Wide Web. The net is made up of the labor of countless anonymous minds that have built this parallel universe, code line by code line. In the short 50 years since its development, netizens have communally built a virtual parallel universe. The small intervention into this universe that constitutes this website is less of a contribution and more a humble attempt to understand its workings and to become more keenly aware of its biases.

Computational tools make the observable world machine-readable. Just as any media that preceded it,computational language becomes the hidden lens through which we access the cacophony of images that come to us every day through our many devices. What we once processed through our experience alone is now transformed into numbers in the form of computational data. “What is chosen as objects, what features are chosen, and how these features are encoded– these three decisions are equally important for representing phenomena as data and, consequently, making them computable, manageable, knowable and shareable through data science techniques” (Lev Manovich in Representing Phenomena as Data”. To code is to be an active participant in  the building of this subjective lens through which we look at the world.

The language and technological ecosystems of the internet have transformed since the 90s and early 2000s, when everyday users would playfully experiment with this new medium. “In 1994, the Internet was still comparatively uncluttered. Populated largely by homepages flaunting hobbies and personal histories, advertising technology companies, or promoting online communities of all stripes, the Net was far removed from the asceticism of white-cube galleries or the high ironies of neo-Conceptualism”. (Rachel Greene in Web Work: A History of Internet Art from Art Forum, 2000)

We now experience a nostalgia for the raw kitsch, the authenticity of those earlier days of net art. Websites are now built around commerce, designed to encourage us to click on the checkout button of the shopping cart. Every code line is studiously written to compete in the economy of attention and commerce. Some of the technologies that allowed everyday people to participate in the construction of the web are slowly becoming obsolete.

The end of flash technology is a notorious but not unique one. “But the software’s end is also a synecdoche of an aesthetic project years in the making. It’s a reminder of how the web has been cleaned up; how it has been transformed from a messy and amateur space into a glossy and corporate one”(Will Bedingfield in A farewell to Adobe Flash— and the Messy, Glorious Web. Wired January 05,2021)

​Nonetheless, we should not be prisoners to a dispiriting nostalgia. While media ecosystems change, so do new avenues for creation arise. There are also many who have archived and shared countless tools that allow us to continue building the web in the spirit it was once: openly generous, participatory, and above all, freaking cool!
